7 Authority
Leaks Costing
Competent Men
the Room

These are not personality flaws. They are signal failures: specific, correctable behaviours that quietly drain the authority you have already earned.
"You don't have a confidence problem.
You have a signal problem."
7
Before you begin
The men who lose rooms are rarely the least capable in them. They are the ones whose signals contradict their substance.
What follows is a precise inventory of the seven most common authority leaks I observe in professional men. Compiled from years of studying presence, frame, and the micro-signals that determine how power is read in a room before a single word is spoken.

Read each one slowly. Notice which ones produce a quiet recognition. That recognition is data.

Most men will identify two or three with certainty. The more dangerous leaks are the ones you cannot see in yourself, and that is precisely the nature of a signal failure.
01
The Filled Silence
Vocal / Frame Control
The inability to inhabit a quiet room is one of the most visible authority leaks in professional settings. When silence descends, after a question, after a challenge, after bad news, most men rush to fill it. They qualify, they soften, they over-explain. The silence reveals their discomfort, and that discomfort reads as a crack in authority.
The signal others receive
"He needs the room's approval to feel safe. He cannot hold tension."
What it costs you: Negotiating leverage. The ability to hold a position under pressure. The perception that your words have weight when you do choose to speak.
02
Upward Inflection on Statements
Vocal / Certainty Signal
When declarative statements end on a rising tone, as though every sentence is a question seeking permission, they undermine the content entirely. It does not matter how correct your position is. Upward inflection turns assertions into appeals, and appeals into requests for validation from whoever holds the higher frame in the room.
The signal others receive
"He is telling me, but asking me at the same time. He is not certain of his own ground."
What it costs you: Credibility under scrutiny. The ability to land a point cleanly. Your arguments are subconsciously processed as hypotheses, not positions.
03
Exit Intention in the Body
Physical / Spatial Authority
Lower body restlessness, weight shifting, feet angled toward the door, legs crossed away from the room's centre of gravity. Each of these communicates a desire to leave before the mind has consciously formed the thought. The body is always speaking. When it signals exit, the room reads it as disengagement, discomfort, or low investment. None of these project authority.
The signal others receive
"He is already somewhere else. He does not fully own this space."
What it costs you: Perceived commitment. The gravitational pull of a man who occupies his space fully. That is one of the most immediate and subconscious authority cues available to you.
04
Visible Costume vs. Visual Armour
Visual / Signal Architecture
Clothing worn without understanding functions as a costume. Clothing worn with precision, cut, cloth and proportion calibrated to the frame and the context,functions as armour. The difference is immediately legible to anyone reading the room. A suit that does not fit correctly, or that has not been chosen with intent, announces that the wearer has not yet taken full responsibility for the visual signal he is producing.
The signal others receive
"He has dressed for the occasion rather than for the position. He is approximating authority, not inhabiting it."
What it costs you: The first thirty seconds of any room. Credibility before you have spoken. The subconscious authority premium that precise visual presence commands.
05
Over-Explanation After a Decision
Verbal / Frame Stability
When a decision has been made and communicated, the authority move is to let it stand. Continuing to justify, soften, or rationalise after the fact signals that the decision is still open, that the right amount of pressure or dissatisfaction could reopen it. This is a frame leak. It invites challenge precisely by trying to pre-empt it.
The signal others receive
"He needs me to agree with him. The decision is not as settled as he wants it to appear."
What it costs you: Decisional authority. Respect from direct reports and peers. The room's confidence that decisions made by you will hold under pressure.
06
Reactive Eye Contact
Physical / Dominance Signal
Most men have been told to maintain eye contact, and most comply until the moment it becomes uncomfortable. The break almost always goes in the same direction: down and away, the submissive break. Reactive eye contact that holds when comfortable and retreats under social pressure is not neutral. It is a signal of conditional confidence, which is read as a ceiling on authority.
The signal others receive
"His confidence is conditional. Apply pressure and it moves."
What it costs you: The ability to hold your ground in confrontational moments. Perceived certainty under challenge. The quiet authority of a man who does not need to look away.
07
Laughing at the Wrong Moment
Social / Frame Compliance
Nervous laughter, appeasing smiles, and reflexive amusement at things that are not genuinely funny are among the most corrosive authority leaks in social and professional settings. They are frame compliance, the body's attempt to reduce tension by signalling submission. The higher the stakes of the interaction, the more damaging this leak becomes, because it occurs precisely when authority most needs to hold.
The signal others receive
"He is managing the room's comfort rather than owning it. His reactions are for them, not from him."
What it costs you: Presence in high-stakes moments. The distinction between a man who owns a room and a man who is trying to survive it.
